*This is a revised and shortened version of an essay I wrote for a Philosophy course back in November of 2019. I've edited it slightly, but it's more or less unchanged...*

Freedom of speech is one of humanity's most sacred and cherished rights. In Canada, we can turn to Section 2(b) of the **Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedom** for a constitutional definition of freedom of speech - “freedom of thought, belief, opinion and expression, including freedom of the press and other media of communication”.

If the government were to prevent speech, like Catharine Mackinnon proposes, it would lead to great political unrest[^1]. Like the death penalty, we must ask ourselves if the State should be given that power to override certain freedoms. The State doesn’t have the competency to determine what should be considered valid speech. Giving the ability to decide what is and isn’t considered offensive speech to the State is quite clearly too much power. By preventing people from expressing a certain view, it signals the government is pleasuring a view that is aligned with their own interests. This has been demonstrated to cause great harm throughout history. 

> “Freedom of speech does not exist to protect popular speech, but to protect unpopular speech. Freedom of speech means freedom to hate so long as it doesn’t directly call for violence”. - [1791](https://www.youtube.com/c/1791dow/featured) 

The State does not have the right to infringe upon the people’s freedom of speech. Language is relative to the time, place, and person using it - as these variables change, so does the intention and context of the language. The complexity and inconsistency of these variables mean the government would be unable to limit and regulate speech effectively to meet Mackinnon’s ends. 

To the degree we limit freedom of speech we put the ability to critically analyze and speak at risk. People become more conscious and get mentally tougher when they voluntarily open themselves up to things they don’t like, Jeremy Waldron referred to this idea as *tolerance*[^2]. If certain views are not being questioned, how would they evolve? People don’t develop when they are shadowed from different ideas, likewise ideas do not develop when people are restricted from criticizing them. By allowing people to fully express a view, and allowing that view to be criticized is to fully challenge all thoughts and ideas. This is the cornerstone of a free marketplace of ideas, which allows ideas based on rationale to develop and those based on genuine hate to be extinguished. 

Mackinnon’s view is also discriminatory by denying people the use of their rationale and autonomy[^2]. What is considered insulting is so subjective, even if the government were to enforce negative infringement, it might not suffice. The nature of language, and subsequently speech, is that it morphs and changes as words and beliefs go in and out of fashion. The cycle of words is harbored by the autonomy of the individual. It is a process that is too sacred to be managed by the state. In the case that it did try to regulate this freedom - in practice it would be like trying to catch smoke with your hands. Mackinnon’s arguments for restricting offensive speech is an over exaggeration of the harm caused by free ideas in a multicultural society. 

> “Sensitivity is not trumps in this game; the stakes are too high for that” - Jermey Waldron[^1]

It is only when offensive ideas are unrestricted that they can be properly criticized. Equality cannot be achieved by forcing people to not fully express a view. Doing so would strengthen their belief in their own personal ideology. Ideas and people help develop one another - stifling ideas you don’t agree with, in the name of equality, will ultimately serve no one.  

Freedom of speech is not a right that can be restricted by the state. To sacrifice that right to the state is to give them too much power - power they are both too incompetent to distribute and not reluctant enough to handle.
